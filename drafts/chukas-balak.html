The Power of Exposing the Snake

“Aseh lecha saraf v’sim oso al nes.” Make for yourself a serpent and place it on a pole. (21:8)


The Bnei Yisrael were bitten by poisonous snakes. People were dying. Moshe davened, and Hashem gave him a strange instruction: make a copper snake and put it on a high pole. Anyone who looks up at it will be healed.


The Mishnah (Rosh Hashanah 3:8) asks the obvious question: does a snake heal? Of course not. Rather, when they looked upward and submitted their hearts to their Father in Heaven, they were healed. But if it was about looking up toward Hashem, why put a snake on the pole at all? Put up an empty pole pointing to shomayim. Why the snake?


Rav Avigdor Miller zt”l explains that the answer goes all the way back to Bereishis. The nachash in Gan Eden represents the Yetzer Hara. It was a cunning being whose job was to make Adam and Chava feel dissatisfied, to convince them that they didn’t have enough, that what they were missing was more important than everything they had. And when Hashem “punished” the nachash by sentencing it to crawl on its belly, that wasn’t really a punishment. It was Hashem establishing the Yetzer Hara’s method forever: stealth.


A snake that had legs, like a rabbit, you’d see coming from a distance. You’d protect yourself. But a snake that crawls on its belly through the grass is almost invisible. It’s colored like copper, nechoshes, which is the same root as nachash, because the color lets it blend into the background. It creeps silently, camouflaged among the leaves and twigs, and by the time you realize it’s there, you’ve already been bitten.


That, says Rav Miller, is exactly how the Yetzer Hara operates. He is the one entity in this world that does not look for kavod. Everybody wants recognition. Everybody likes publicity. But the Yetzer Hara prefers to remain unseen, because that’s his greatest tool. He crawls into your thoughts and you don’t even notice the change. He’s so well disguised among your own thinking that you believe the thoughts are yours.


This is why Hashem told Moshe to put the snake on a HIGH POLE. It’s the exact opposite of what the Yetzer Hara wants. He wants to stay as low as possible, hidden in the grass, invisible. “Don’t talk about me,” the Yetzer Hara says. “I’m an anav. I don’t want publicity. My success is incognito.” And Hashem says: “Put him on a pole. Lift him up high. Let everybody see.”


Moshe wasn’t just performing a miracle. He was giving the Am Yisrael a shiur. He was saying: “Look at this copper-colored snake that has been camouflaged among your thoughts. I’m lifting it up for you to see it for what it is. The dissatisfaction you feel, the sense that you’d be better off somewhere else, the craving for what you don’t have instead of gratitude for what you do, it’s not real. It’s the nachash. And the moment you see it clearly, it loses its power over you.”


For those of us in this struggle, this is one of the most powerful ideas in the Torah.


The Yetzer Hara of our particular battle is a master of camouflage. He doesn’t come to you and say, “Go sin.” He crawls in quietly. He sounds like your own voice. “Just a quick look.” “It’s not that bad.” “Everyone does it.” “You’ve had a hard day, you deserve this.” “You’ll start fresh tomorrow.” By the time you recognize what’s happening, you’re already bitten. That’s his whole strategy: stay hidden, stay silent, blend in with the background of your thoughts so you never realize that the impulse isn’t yours at all. It’s the nachash talking.


And that is exactly why the most effective tool in recovery is the one the Yetzer Hara fears most: exposure.


This is actually the third pillar of what GYE is built on: connection. And one of the most powerful benefits of connection, one that people don’t always realize, isn’t just the support or the advice. It’s the fact that simply being open with someone already weakens the desire. When you post on the forum and say, “I’m struggling, here’s what happened,” you’re lifting the nachash out of the grass and holding it up for everyone to see. When you write in the GYE chat and share what’s going on, when you find a mentor and tell him what you’re going through, the Yetzer Hara’s camouflage is stripped away. He’s no longer invisible. He’s just a snake, and someone else can see him too.


That’s why the Yetzer Hara fights so hard to keep you silent. “Don’t tell anyone.” “They’ll think less of you.” “You can handle this on your own.” “It’s too embarrassing.” Every one of those thoughts is the nachash trying to stay in the grass. Because the moment you expose him, his power shrinks.


Rav Miller also brings the pasuk from Mishlei (17:24): “Einei ksil b’ktzei ha’aretz,” the eyes of a fool are at the ends of the earth. The fool always thinks happiness is somewhere else. Over there, the grass is greener. Over there, life is better. That other person has it easier. If only I had a different situation, a different wife, a different life. And this, says Rav Miller, is one of the nachash’s favorite tricks: making you blind to what you already have by keeping your eyes fixed on what you don’t.


These habits and behaviors work the same way. They keep your eyes on the screen, on the fantasy, on the imaginary life that doesn’t exist, while your real life, your wife, your children, your learning, your connection to Hashem, sits right in front of you, unseen and unappreciated. The nachash doesn’t just tempt you toward something bad. He blinds you to everything good.


The copper snake on the pole is the antidote. Look at it. See the Yetzer Hara for what he is. Name him. Expose him. Talk about him. And then look past him, upward, toward Hashem, and toward the life that’s already yours.


The Gemara (Kiddushin 30b) says: “If Hakadosh Baruch Hu wouldn’t help a man, he couldn’t overcome the Yetzer Hara.” You can’t do this alone. But the Gemara also says that if a person is too arrogant to ask for help, Hashem leaves him in the hands of his Yetzer Hara. Asking for help isn’t weakness. It’s the ultimate act of putting the snake on the pole. It’s saying, “I see you, nachash. I know what you are. And I’m not fighting you alone anymore.”


That’s recovery. Not more willpower. Not gritting your teeth harder. Exposing the snake. Lifting him up where everyone can see. And watching his power disappear in the light.

Two Years Is a Level That I Never Dreamed of Reaching

By @hopefulfalcon72 (Current streak: 730 days)


I don’t know what to say. I don’t know how I did it. All I can say is I had help from Hashem.


Before this, my longest streak ever was three weeks. And then I was in overnight camp and I spoke to an older guy about everything I was going through, because I was at three weeks in this streak, and I have found that that is the hardest part. Just speaking to him really helped.


I found that being open about it really helps, because there’s nothing to be ashamed of.


But this feels different. Two years is a different level that I never even dreamed of reaching, and it doesn’t feel real.


There’s not much more to say. What worked for me was davening that Hashem helps me fight this and focusing on the good days, not the bad ones. Because if you focus on the bad ones, you can get stuck in a dark place that’s hard to get out of.

Wisdom from the Forum: “How Do I Get Through the Summer When the Routine Disappears?”

Summer is the snake’s favorite season. The routine that kept you safe all winter, the schedule, the structure, the daily rhythm, it all falls apart. Bein hazmanim. Kids home from school. Wife upstate. More free time. More time alone. More triggers on the street. The Yetzer Hara doesn’t take vacation. He waits for the moment your guard drops, and summer hands him that moment on a silver platter.


The forum has been talking about this for years, and the advice is remarkably consistent.


Plan before the summer hits you.


One member committed to updating his “summer forum” daily, or as much as possible, because he knew that without structure, the days would blur together and the cracks would widen. He writes: “Every day is a challenge. Every day clean is a day won.” @inastruggle suggests posting “once or twice a week to keep yourself accountable.” The accountability doesn’t have to be complicated. It just has to exist.


The practical reality is that summer changes the battlefield. More free time, more pritzus on the streets, more access to devices when you’re alone. Members consistently say that the key is to prepare before it starts. One member emphasizes the need to “constantly prepare myself for where I am going and what I might be up against.” Don’t walk into the summer blind. Know your danger zones and have a plan for each one.


Guard your eyes, literally.


This is where summer gets specific. @fulfillinglife613 shares a technique that’s simple but powerful: “I focus on not looking AGAIN. If I saw it, I saw it, but by consciously not looking again I’m also reminding myself that I need to refocus my brain and concentrate on something else.” You can’t control the first glance. You can control the second one.


@machshovo_tova takes it a step further: “Before you go out, daven to Hashem, ‘Ha’aveir einai meir’os shav,’ and in your own words tell Him how helpless you are without His help, and ask Him to help you avoid immodest sights.” Making that tefillah before you walk out the door changes the entire dynamic. You’re not white-knuckling through the street. You’re walking with Hashem.


@vehkam offers a practical hack: “Headphones and good music or shiurim work wonders to remove me from the surrounding environment.” It sounds simple, but it creates a mental barrier between you and the street. When your ears are occupied with Torah or music, your eyes are less hungry.


@shivisi_hashem adds a beautiful reframe: “Just think about Hashem’s wonders you can find all over the street, starting from the clouds, trees, humans, animals, kids jumping around, and many many more. They will distract you from the lust of the street.” Instead of fighting not to look at the wrong things, fill your eyes with the right things.


Don’t isolate.


This is the big one for summer. When the wife is in the country and you’re in the city, or the kids are in camp and the house is quiet, isolation becomes the Yetzer Hara’s best friend. @otr_otr learned the hard way: “Had I continued keeping myself out of isolation, I would have probably remained in recovery for longer.” @yedidyaaleph suggests dedicated summer threads on the forum, even evening phone meetings, to maintain connection when the regular support structure is gone.


Keep perspective.


@larry_bird offers a word that’s worth taping to your bathroom mirror: “Don’t get too hard on yourself with the ups and the downs, as long as you are taking one step that is somewhat out of your comfort zone in the path of recovery.” Summer isn’t about perfection. It’s about staying in the fight. And as one member reminds us: “Keeping strong in the summer is the result of the hard-working days in the winter.” Everything you’ve built doesn’t disappear because the calendar changed. It’s still there. You just have to protect it through a more challenging season.


The bottom line? Summer is when the snake comes out of the grass and onto the sidewalk. The routine that kept you safe isn’t there to protect you. So you build a summer routine. You daven before you walk out the door. You wear headphones. You post on the forum. You don’t isolate. And you remember that every single day you get through clean is a day won, no matter how hot it gets outside.

10 Really Dumb Mistakes Smart Couples Make

Mistake #4: Respect First, By Rabbi Benzion Shafier (TheShmuz.com)


Imagine two not-so-similar scenarios.


A young couple is walking down the street. He trips. Immediately, she says, “Oy! Are you okay? Are you hurt?”


That’s scenario one. Here’s scenario two:


The same man and woman are walking down the street. He trips. This time her response is, “What’s wrong with you, klutz?”


What’s the difference between these two scenarios? The first one is when they were chassan and kallah. The second one is when they’ve been married for three years.


The Rambam’s Formula

The Rambam (Hilchos Ishus 15:19) gives us the formula for a successful marriage in two short statements: “Our Sages commanded that a husband must honor his wife more than himself and he must love her as much as himself. Likewise, they commanded a wife to treat her husband with exceeding amounts of honor, treating him as if he were nobility. If a couple does this, their union will be beautiful and praiseworthy.”


Notice the order. He has to honor his wife more than himself, and love her like himself. Honor comes first.


But didn’t we just spend an entire chapter explaining that love is the glue of marriage? That without it the relationship falls apart? Why does the Rambam put honor before love?


Why Respect Comes First

In the secular world, the focus of relationships is always on love. Love determines who you should marry, love is what keeps you together, love is everything. Yet most of those relationships fail. Because people use the word “love” when they really mean desire, infatuation, or attraction. They mistake the engine for the car.


Real love, the Rambam is teaching us, can only exist in a climate of respect. Without respect, love withers. A wife who doesn’t feel respected by her husband can’t feel loved by him either. A husband who is constantly criticized can’t feel his wife’s love through the criticism.


The “I Love Fish” Problem

Rav Yehuda Leib Chasman zt”l, the Mashgiach of Yeshivas Chevron, once saw a talmid eating fish with a bit too much gusto. He said to the talmid, “I see you love fish.” The talmid said sheepishly, “Yes, Rebbi. I love fish.” Reb Leib Chasman replied, “You don’t love fish. If you loved the fish, you would dress it up, take care of it, and make sure it was well fed. You don’t love fish. You love yourself.”


This is the fundamental mistake people make about love. When a young couple falls in “love,” each of them sees in the other someone who will meet all of their needs. They’re focused on what they’re getting. But that’s not love. Love is giving. Love is being concerned for the good of another.


And giving can only happen in a climate of respect. If you don’t respect your wife, your “giving” becomes transactional, self-serving, or patronizing. Respect is the soil in which real love grows. Without it, nothing takes root.


What Disrespect Looks Like

Disrespect doesn’t always look like name-calling or shouting. Sometimes it’s subtle. Rolling your eyes when she talks. Checking your phone while she’s telling you about her day. Dismissing her concerns as overreactions. Making decisions without consulting her. These small acts of disrespect accumulate, and over time they erode the foundation of the marriage.


The Rambam says to honor your wife “more than yourself.” More. Not equally. More. Because when you treat her with that level of kavod, she feels safe, valued, and seen. And in that environment, love doesn’t just survive. It deepens.


The Fourth Really Dumb Mistake

This is the fourth really dumb mistake smart couples make: they let respect slip. They would never speak to a colleague the way they speak to their spouse. They would never dismiss a friend’s feelings the way they dismiss their wife’s. Somehow, the person who matters most gets the least respect.


The chassan and kallah treated each other with honor because they saw each other clearly. Three years later, familiarity bred carelessness. The respect faded, and without it, the love couldn’t breathe.


The fix isn’t complicated. It’s a decision. Decide that your wife deserves at least the same respect you’d give a stranger. Then decide she deserves more. The Rambam says more than yourself, and if the greatest halachic authority in history thought that was important enough to codify, it’s probably worth taking seriously.


Your Action Steps This Week

1. Pay attention to how you speak to your wife this week. Would you use that tone with a colleague? If not, adjust.


2. The next time she tells you about something that matters to her, put your phone down, make eye contact, and listen. Not to respond. To hear.


3. Find one moment this week to honor her publicly, in front of the children, in front of friends, in front of family. Let her hear you speak about her the way you did when you were chassan and kallah.
