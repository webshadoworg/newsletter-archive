Comfort Before You’ve Earned It

There’s a word in this week’s haftarah that should stop us in our tracks.


“Nachamu nachamu ami.” Comfort, comfort My people.


Notice the timing. This is said immediately after the churban, immediately after the worst has happened. And Hashem doesn’t say “straighten out and maybe I’ll comfort you.” He doesn’t say “earn your way back first.” He says: My people. Right now, in the ash and the wreckage, you are still His.


That’s the nechama of Shabbos Nachamu. The comfort comes before you’ve earned it.


The double language - why nachamu twice? One answer: the first nachamu addresses the external loss, the destruction, the exile. The second addresses something harder - the inner sense that you have drifted too far to find your way back. The voice that whispers: this is who I am. I’m not the type who recovers. The second nachamu exists specifically to answer that voice: yes you are. Yes you can. You are still His.


And then look at the parsha itself. Moshe prayed 515 times to enter the land - the word va’etchanan, his intense pleading, carries that numerical value. He poured his whole self into those prayers. And he still didn’t get in. What happened to all of those tefillos? The Medrash says: they weren’t lost. They were stored. No prayer returns empty.


Every time you reached out in a dark moment - every “Hashem, help me,” every time you got up after a fall and tried again, every broken attempt - that didn’t evaporate. It accumulated. Even when you didn’t see a result. Even when you fell again the next week. The effort was stored.


The same parsha that records Moshe’s unanswered prayer also gives us the Shema. “Hashem Elokeinu Hashem Echad.” The oneness of Hashem means there is no place He is not. No depth you can sink to where He isn’t already there, waiting. The Shema is the antidote to the feeling that you have gone somewhere beyond His reach.


You haven’t.


Bein hazmanim is a tough stretch for many of our members. The routines that hold you fall away, the structure loosens, and for many people this is when the hardest moments happen. So take this with you into the next few weeks: Hashem doesn’t wait for the schedule to return before He shows up. The comfort doesn’t arrive after you’ve cleaned yourself up. It comes first - in the middle of the hardest stretch, before you’ve earned it.


Nachamu nachamu ami. You’re still His. Now let’s go.

Bein Hazmanim Tips From a Fighter

By @kerby (Current streak: 277 days)


The struggle sure is real.


Bein hazmanim is a tough time. I think you need to:


First, be accountable. Have someone you’ll speak to or text every single day.


Second, do everything you can to make your environment kosher. That means filtering your devices, for real. Surround yourself with guys you can grow from. Make a seder kavua. And try not to have too much free time. Keep busy.


And lastly, daven hard. At the end of the day, the only One who can really help you is Hashem. So speak to Him in whatever language you’d like, and daven with real kavana. “Hashem gives His loved ones tzaros because He wants to hear their voice.”

Wisdom from the Forum: “How Do I Stay Clean When I Can’t Control the Devices Around Me?”

This is one of the toughest spots to be in, common for singles especially: you can’t filter other people’s devices, you’re too embarrassed to ask them to, and yet the temptation is sitting right there within reach. It’s a real challenge, and plenty of members have wrestled with it. Here’s what the chevra has to say.


The first thing the community will gently push back on is the framing itself. @mottehpoisansky makes a sharp point: as much as the question is about devices, “it’s not about the devices, it’s that he’s out of control.” In other words, the unfiltered phone in someone else’s hand is a real problem, but the deeper work is internal. Devices are the battlefield; they aren’t the war.


That said, the community strongly believes in doing everything you can control, starting with your own devices. @freeeyes613, after a slip, was told plainly: “Make sure to install a strong filter. It will be almost impossible to break free of this while having all the garbage within a mouse click away.” Even people who could easily beat a filter still swear by having one. @i-man, a yungerman in kollel, admits that when the tayveh was strong enough he’d find ways around his filters, and @porrtw, an IT professional who can bypass any filter he wants, still came to the forum asking how to block himself. The lesson isn’t that filters are useless - it’s that a filter buys you time and friction, and you pair it with the inner work. @DeletedUser845, mostly clean for over 500 days, sums up the practical setup: “I have filters everywhere, and I never sit alone with an unfiltered device. I don’t have a smartphone, so much less triggers like that, BH.” That single rule - never be alone with an unfiltered device - is one of the most protective habits you can build when you can’t control the environment.


If the uncontrolled device is at home, several members have faced exactly that. @rt234 was in real distress over an unfiltered device in the house but felt he couldn’t tell his parents because they “won’t know how to handle the situation.” @taharat yisrael offered a courageous answer: if you’re living at home, you may need to work up the nerve to raise it with your parents, since they often simply don’t realize the dangers of unfiltered access. It’s an awkward conversation, but you may be protecting more people than just yourself.


When you’re caught in the moment with no filter to lean on, @mr.clean is honest that sometimes early on you just have to “white knuckle it, tough it out, put a T-shirt in your mouth and bite down hard and wait for it to pass.” But he’s just as clear that this isn’t a long-term plan, because “white knuckling can only get you so far.” The goal is to graduate out of gritting your teeth. @DeletedUser845 describes what the other side looks like: “I’m no longer biting my nails and white-knuckling; even when I get an urge, I usually just let it pass.” That shift - from fighting every urge to simply letting it move through you - comes with time and the deeper work.


And that work starts with action, not just resolve. @peloni almoni puts it bluntly: “Deciding doesn’t ever really do much. It is acting upon the decision that yields results.” Kabbalos alone tend not to hold. @inastruggle found that before GYE his resolutions to stop never stuck, and that actually posting and interacting with the community was the thing that changed the game. @onthewayup uses the forum the same way, as a source of accountability to carry him through the hard stretches. When you can’t control the devices, you can control whether you’re doing this alone.


It also helps enormously to know your own patterns, because the times you’re most exposed are usually predictable. @iabsolutelycan noticed that one fall came right after “patting myself on the back on how good I was,” and another came from staying up late to work - “when you’re tired, the Yetzer Hara is not.” @david26fr points to extra stress and unfulfilled ambition as common triggers, and @Embarrassed Husband knows the niddah periods are his most vulnerable stretch. If you can name when you’re weakest, you can plan not to be alone with a device precisely then.


Finally, the longest-term answer is the biggest reframe. @dd suggests turning the whole question around: instead of focusing only on falling or not falling, ask “what can you do when you’re clean to really change your attitude, on a much broader level, like how to really live life in a happy way.” When your life is full and you’re genuinely content, the pull of the device in someone else’s hand loses a lot of its power.


The bottom line? You may not be able to filter the world around you, but you’re far from powerless. Lock down your own devices, refuse to sit alone with an unfiltered one, find the courage to speak up at home if you can, lean hard on the chevra for accountability, learn your danger times, and build a life happy enough that the temptation shrinks. It’s a continuous battle, but as members here put it, you fight it with chizuk from your brothers in arms, and you never fight it alone.

10 Really Dumb Mistakes Smart Couples Make

Mistake #8: Judging Your Spouse Based on Your Own Experience, By Rabbi Benzion Shafier (TheShmuz.com)


Last week we saw that men and women talk for different reasons. This week goes one layer deeper, to the quiet assumption underneath almost every marital argument: that my experience of the world is simply reality, and that my spouse should see it the same way.


Up on the Chair


Rabbi Shafier tells the story of Moshe, married about six months, walking home thinking how lucky he is to have married such a grounded, put-together woman, not one of those emotional types. He opens the door and finds his put-together wife standing on a chair, shrieking. “Sara, what’s wrong?” “There! Over there!” He looks. “What?” “A... a... a COCKROACH!” He walks over, steps on it, and says, “You can come down now.”


Two weeks later he’s learning in the beis medrash and his phone rings. “Moshe! Come home!” “What happened, is everything okay?” “THERE ARE TWO OF THEM! PLEASE COME HOME.” He drives home fuming, stomps on both bugs, turns to his wife and says, “I hope you’re satisfied,” and storms back to the beis medrash.


Who’s Right?


So who’s right? Was she right to call her husband home because she was frightened and needed help? Was he right to be upset at being pulled away from learning for something so trivial? Some will side with her - how could he be so callous? Some will side with him - how could she have so little regard for his learning?


The right answer, says Rabbi Shafier, is that the question itself is wrong. “Who is right?” is probably the single most damaging question you can ask in a marriage. It’s a fine question for a courtroom or a labor dispute. It has no place between spouses. A marriage is a union of two unique people, each with their own needs, wishes, and ways of seeing the world, and very often those needs compete. There’s only so much time, money, and energy to go around. There will be many times you don’t get what you want. A marriage works not when someone wins, but when both people know their feelings count.


The Better Question


So instead of “who’s right?”, the productive question is: what is each person asking for? Climb into their inner worlds. Up on that chair, Sara was thinking: Baruch Hashem, now that I’m married I don’t have to face these dreadful things alone, my best friend will come save me. Then he kills the bug, gives her a disgusted look, and says “I hope you’re satisfied,” and she thinks: I was genuinely scared and he doesn’t even care. He’s making me feel like a stupid little kid. Meanwhile he’s thinking: I can’t believe she called me home from learning to kill a bug. Why couldn’t she just step on it herself? Two people hurting each other, and neither understands why the other is hurt, because each is trapped inside their own reality.


It’s Never Really the Issue


This is the key insight: when couples fight, they think they’re fighting about the issue - the money, the bug, the schedule. They’re not. They’re fighting about what the issue means to each of them, and they’re each judging the other by their own emotional reality. Many women are afraid of bugs; many men aren’t. To Sara, a large bug with lots of legs is genuinely terrifying. To Moshe, it’s nothing. He judged her by how he feels (“bugs are no big deal, she’s overreacting”). She judged him by how she feels (“everyone knows bugs are scary, he’s heartless”).


Rabbi Shafier flips the scene to make it land. Imagine Moshe alone in the apartment when two large German shepherds come charging through the door. He’s not standing on a chair - he’s out the fire escape and down the block. In his world, dogs are scary and bugs aren’t. The moment he lets his own experience define reality, he can’t understand his wife, because in her world, bugs really are frightening.


This is the eighth really dumb mistake smart couples make: judging your spouse based on your own experience, and letting your experience define reality. If you judge your spouse by the way you feel, living together in peace becomes nearly impossible, because sooner or later what they’re doing will make “no sense” to you.


Mind Blind


This isn’t only a marriage problem, it’s a universal human handicap. Rabbi Shafier calls it “mind blindness”: the failure to grasp that what I know, or feel, isn’t automatically what you know or feel. He illustrates it with a famous experiment. A Stanford researcher, Elizabeth Newton, split people into “tappers” and “listeners.” Tappers tapped out the rhythm of a well-known song, Happy Birthday, the national anthem, and guessed how often the listener would identify it. They predicted about fifty-fifty. In reality, only about 2.5% of listeners guessed right. Why did the tappers expect so much? Because as they tapped, they could hear the full song playing in their own heads, and they couldn’t imagine the listener didn’t hear it too. That’s mind blindness. And recognizing that the song playing in your head isn’t playing in your spouse’s head is essential to living with another person.


Creamer in the Coffee


Rabbi Shafier gives a tender example from his own marriage. Most Shabbos mornings he brings his wife coffee in bed, a routine of many years. And almost every time, he reaches for the creamer and has to catch himself, because she takes skim milk. In his experience, coffee is obviously better with creamer - smoother, richer. So his instinct is to give her “the best.” But the best, by his definition, is what he likes. Loving her well means pouring the skim milk - the coffee she wants, not the coffee he’d want for himself. That’s the whole mistake in miniature: even our kindness can be secretly built around our own experience.


The repair is to accept, deeply, that your spouse is a genuinely different person, of the opposite gender no less, with their own nature, temperament, and inner reality, and that once you’re married, you’re responsible for the emotional climate between you. You don’t have to fix them or agree with them. You do have to learn to see the world through their eyes.


Your Action Steps This Week


  1. The next time you feel the “how can they not see this?” frustration rising, stop and swap the question. Don’t ask who’s right. Ask: what is my spouse actually feeling, and what are they asking for?
  2. Find one small thing you do “for” your spouse that’s secretly built around your own preference - your version of the creamer - and do it their way this week instead.
  3. When you disagree, try saying out loud: “Help me understand how this feels from where you’re sitting.” Then just listen, without defending your own version of reality.
