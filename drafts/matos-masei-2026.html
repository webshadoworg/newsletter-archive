What Kept Them Clean

In this week’s parsha, Moshe sends 12,000 soldiers to war against Midian. They return and report: “Not one man of us is missing” (31:49). The simple meaning is that not a single soldier was killed. But the Gemara says that it means something deeper -- that not one man sinned (see Rashbi in Yevamos 61a; Ramban on the Posuk, Rashi Shir Hashirim 4:2).


This is striking. Just a short time earlier, the Jewish people had fallen into a major trap, at the very same place. The Midianite women had enticed them, and 24,000 died as a result. Now 12,000 soldiers march straight into Midian - and not one stumbles. What changed?


The answer may lie in the difference between a man who is idle and a man who is on a mission. The previous generation fell because they were hanging out - vayeshev Yisrael b’Shittim, the Jewish people settled in Shittim. Settled. Idle. No particular place to be. The Midianite women found them in that vacuum, and the vacuum pulled. The 12,000 soldiers were different. They had a specific mission. They knew where they were going, what they were there to do, and what it meant to win. They cared about it. And that sense of purpose changed the entire inner landscape.


The Yetzer Hara still showed up - it always does. But it was competing with something real, something the soldiers were genuinely invested in. And it lost.


This gives us a three-level framework for our own struggle:


Level 1 - Redirect your attention. When an urge hits, narrow your focus back to what’s in front of you - shopping, driving, whatever it is. Give it your full attention. This isn’t distraction; it’s crowding out the urge by leaving it no open space to expand into.


Level 2 - Focus on something meaningful. Beyond technique: be working toward something that genuinely matters to you. A seder for the morning, a seder for the night, a day structured around goals you’re actually excited about. When you carry a bigger picture in your head - something you’re building, something you want to reach - the Yetzer Hara has to compete with something real. You don’t get lost when you know where you’re going and you actually want to get there.


Level 3 - See it as war. The deepest shift. Chazal teach: hu Yetzer Hara, hu Satan, hu Malach HaMaves - one force, three names. When you recognize the urge not as a craving but as your enemy - the one trying to destroy your marriage, your neshamah, your life - you stop negotiating. The 12,000 soldiers didn’t debate whether to engage. This time, they went in clear-eyed, knowing exactly who the enemy was. And they came home with not one man missing.

Wisdom from the Forum: “How Do I Stay Clean While I’m Traveling Away From Home?”

Fittingly for Masei, the parsha of the forty-two journeys, this week’s question is about the road. Traveling is one of the toughest tests members face. It’s a tremendous nisayon: a hotel room to yourself, no routine, no one watching. Here’s what the community has learned about staying strong on the road.


The single most repeated piece of advice is to decide before you leave. Mentally prepare for the trip and commit to staying clean, facing each challenge one nisayon at a time. Don’t wait until you’re alone in the room at 11 p.m. to figure out your plan. Walk in knowing where the danger points are and what you’ll do when they come.


Isolation is the enemy, so several members do whatever they can to avoid a solo room. @cordnoy put it plainly: “I have something in a similar city in several months’ time. As of now, I will only go if I figure out a way to bring my wife with me.” @godhelp said the same about an upcoming trip to Vegas: “I think I will take my wife along.” It’s not always practical, @reallygettingthere pointed out that bringing your wife is hard when there are kids at home, so if that’s not an option, @blackbigday offers the next best thing: “Can you set yourself up with a male roommate?” Either way, the goal is the same, don’t spend the trip alone.


On the road, the ordinary rules matter even more. Limit socializing with women to only what is honestly necessary, and stay connected to your shmiras einayim chizuk so your eyes stay trained even in an unfamiliar environment. Small boundaries, kept consistently, are what hold when the structure of home is gone.


Travel doesn’t create new vulnerabilities so much as amplify existing ones. @alexeliezer made a sharp observation: sometimes the fear of traveling comes from losing a “safer” outlet you’d quietly been relying on to avoid something worse, which is worth being honest with yourself about. For married men, being away during niddah periods can stack one vulnerable time on top of another. And @colincolin noted that falls are often driven by “extra stress in your life, or unfulfilled ambition, or work problems,” exactly the things a business trip tends to pile on. @david26fr agreed. Name your triggers before the trip so they don’t ambush you during it.


You can bring the whole community with you in your pocket. The forum itself provides tremendous chizuk, just reading and knowing that others are in the same fight can carry you through a lonely night in a hotel. And keep the frame simple. Even after long stretches of sobriety, members stress the need to take things day by day and never rest on your laurels. On the road, you’re not trying to conquer the whole trip at once. You’re just staying clean today, in this city, in this room.


One technique @HashemHelpMe highly recommends from GYE’s Accountability Partnerships mini-course: bookending. Before you enter a high-risk situation - a business trip, a known trigger, a stretch where you’ve struggled before - contact your accountability partner, share your plan and your commitment. Then follow up afterward to report how it went. It builds accountability around specific challenges rather than just general progress, and on the road, that targeted check-in can be exactly what holds.


The bottom line? Masei reminds us that life is a series of journeys, forty-two stops between Mitzrayim and the Promised Land. Some of those stops are harder than others, and travel is one of them. But the same tools that hold at home hold on the road: prepare in advance, refuse to isolate, guard the small choices, know your triggers, lean on the chevra, and win today. Then get up tomorrow and win again.

10 Really Dumb Mistakes Smart Couples Make

Mistake #6: Trying to Change Your Spouse, By Rabbi Benzion Shafier (TheShmuz.com)


Last week we saw that a woman’s deepest need in marriage is to feel cherished. This week we turn to the parallel truth about men, and to a habit so common, and so well-intentioned, that most people don’t even realize it’s tearing their marriage apart.


The Ten-Point Home Improvement Plan


Rabbi Shafier only half-jokes that the average kallah walks down the aisle with a ten-point home-improvement policy, and the focus of the plan is that fellow standing under the chuppah. She’s patient. She waits a week or so after the wedding. Then, when he can’t find the checkbook, she gently points out that if he were just a bit more organized, it would save him so much time. She even offers to help reorganize his desk.


And to her complete bewilderment, he doesn’t melt with gratitude. In fact, the more she “helps,” the worse it seems to get.


Two Different Worlds


In a woman’s world, helping is one of the highest expressions of love. A good friend notices what you could do better and tells you, because she cares. But a man’s world runs on something different. Men value independence. Self-reliance is a source of pride: I’ve got this. It’s under control. To walk over to a man and offer advice on how to do something better implies that he can’t get the job done himself.


So when a wife offers her husband well-meant advice, she’s speaking the language of love, and he’s hearing the language of disrespect. What she means is “I care about you.” What he hears is “You’re not capable. You’re only half a man.”


The Sixth Really Dumb Mistake


This is the sixth really dumb mistake smart couples make: trying to change your spouse. When you direct, guide, correct, or “improve” your husband, he doesn’t think, Look how much she loves me. He thinks, She believes I’m not good enough. And it cuts both ways. When a man tries to change his wife, what she hears is: I’m not acceptable as I am. If he loved me, he wouldn’t be trying to change me. Trying to change your spouse sends one message, in either direction, “You’re just not good enough”, and no one, of any gender, responds well to that.


Socks on the Floor


A woman once told Rabbi Shafier that her husband, forty years old, still leaves his socks on the bedroom floor no matter how many times she asks. He asked her a series of questions. Is he a nice guy? Yes. Considerate? Yes. Does he help around the house? Yes. “So here’s the hard truth,” he told her. “He may never change. Some people are gifted in one area and sloppy in another. If you’ve asked and asked and it hasn’t helped, you may have to face the fact that this isn’t going to change. And then the real question is: what do you do about it?”


The answer came from a woman in Chicago who heard this same example two years earlier. Her husband used to drape a jacket over a different dining room chair every night until his whole wardrobe was in the dining room by Thursday. She used to scream about it. It never worked. So she tried something else: every night, she quietly took the jacket upstairs and hung it up. “How’s your shalom bayis now?” Rabbi Shafier asked. “Wonderful,” she said.


Is it still his responsibility to hang up his own jacket? Of course. Everybody should be better. But he’s responsible for changing him, and she’s responsible only for her side of the street.


Pushing When You Should Be Pulling


There is one way, the only way, to actually get your spouse to change. Rabbi Shafier tells it as a mashal. A man is locked in a room with cement walls and one door. He pushes. Nothing. He pushes harder, throws his whole body against it, day after day, until he collapses in exhaustion, certain he’ll die trapped in there. Then he looks up and sees, on the bottom of the door, one word: Pull.


Husbands and wives spend years pushing on the door, and it never opens, because they’re pushing when they should be pulling. Pull back. Stop trying to change your spouse and work instead on accepting them exactly as they are. When a husband feels respected and a wife feels cherished, something remarkable happens: both become far more eager to please the other. Does that guarantee your spouse will change? No. Some things never change. But in a climate of respect and love, at least change becomes possible, which is more than pushing will ever get you.


Your Action Steps This Week


  1. Catch yourself once this week, right before you correct, coach, or “improve” your spouse, and simply don’t. Notice what happens instead.
  2. Pick the one habit that bothers you most and quietly decide to stop fighting it. Like the jacket on the chair, just handle it, and watch what it does to the temperature of your home.
  3. When the urge to change your spouse rises, say the line to yourself: My job is to change me. My job is to accept my spouse for who they are. Then keep your eyes on your own side of the mechitzah.
