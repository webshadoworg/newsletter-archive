This Week's Torah Thought

What You Put First Is Who You Are

In this week's parsha, Klal Yisrael stood on the edge of a dream. They had left Mitzrayim, marched to Har Sinai, and received the Torah just weeks later. Ten months after that, they were told it was time to enter the Promised Land. And then, because of the spies, it all collapsed. Forty years in the desert. Imagine the frustration of watching the dream slip away.

So they waited. And waited. A full forty years, until finally the green light came. And right in the middle of all that joy, the tribes of Reuven and Gad said: we'd rather stay on this side of the Jordan.

Their words: "Pens for the flock shall we build here for our livestock, and cities for our small children."

Moshe responded: "Build for yourselves cities for your small children, and pens for your flock."

Reuven and Gad said: livestock first, then children. Moshe said: children first, then livestock. The order is small and enormous at the same time.

In recovery, we learn that the whole battle comes down to the destruction of self-centeredness. And nothing exposes self-centeredness faster than what a person puts first. Livestock represents our material wealth — the possessions, the pleasures, the appetites of the moment. Children represent our spiritual wealth — the future, the legacy, the eternal link we carry forward. Moshe gently flipped the order. He wasn't just correcting their grammar. He was correcting their hearts.

This is the question the parsha puts to each of us. The Yetzer Hara is, at bottom, the voice of "livestock first." It always argues for the here and now. The pleasure in this moment. The craving that feels urgent right now. It never mentions the children, the marriage, the neshamah, the eternal — until after it has already put the material in front.

The work of recovery is learning to consciously reorder the list. To put the children first, the marriage first, the connection to Hashem first, and to let the material take its proper place behind them.

The next time you feel the pull, pause and ask the parsha's question: what comes first? Name what actually matters — your wife, your children, the person you want to be, your connection to Hashem — and put it back at the front of the line where it belongs.

The urge is Reuven and Gad. Your job is to be Moshe, and quietly flip the order.

Your priorities are always showing. What you put first is who you are. The only question is whether the front of your list is pointing at the flock, or at the future.

Wisdom from the Forum

"How Do I Stay Clean While I'm Traveling Away From Home?"

Fittingly for Masei — the parsha of the forty-two journeys — this week's question is about the road. Traveling is one of the toughest tests members face: a hotel room to yourself, no routine, no one watching, and long stretches of boredom that the Yetzer Hara knows exactly how to exploit. Here's what the community has learned.

Prepare Before You Pack

The most-repeated piece of advice: decide before you leave. Mentally commit to staying clean, facing each challenge one nisayon at a time. Don't wait until you're alone in the room at 11 p.m. to figure out your plan. Walk in knowing where the danger points are and what you'll do when they come.

Don't Travel Into Isolation

Isolation is the enemy. Several members do whatever they can to avoid a solo room. @cordnoy: "I will only go if I figure out a way to bring my wife." If that's not an option, @blackbigday offers: "Can you set yourself up with a male roommate?" Either way — don't spend the trip alone.

Keep Your Guard Up in the Small Choices

Limit socializing with women to only what's honestly necessary. Stay connected to your shmiras einayim chizuk so your eyes stay trained even in an unfamiliar environment. Small boundaries, kept consistently, hold when the structure of home is gone.

Know Your Own Weak Points

Travel doesn't create new vulnerabilities — it amplifies existing ones. @colincolin noted that falls are often driven by "extra stress in your life, or unfulfilled ambition, or work problems" — exactly what a business trip piles on. Name your triggers before the trip so they don't ambush you during it.

Lean on the Community & Take It One Day at a Time

You can bring the whole community with you in your pocket. The forum itself provides tremendous chizuk — just reading and knowing that others are in the same fight can carry you through a lonely night. You're not trying to conquer the whole trip at once. You're just staying clean today, in this city, in this room.

Masei reminds us that life is a series of journeys — forty-two stops between Mitzrayim and the Promised Land. Some of those stops are harder than others. But the same tools that hold at home hold on the road: prepare in advance, refuse to isolate, guard the small choices, know your triggers, lean on the chevra, and win today.

10 Really Dumb Mistakes — Mistake #6

Trying to Change Your Spouse

By Rabbi Benzion Shafier • TheShmuz.com

The Ten-Point Home Improvement Plan

Rabbi Shafier only half-jokes that the average kallah walks down the aisle with a ten-point home-improvement policy, and the focus of the plan is that fellow standing under the chuppah. She's patient. She waits a week or so after the wedding. Then, when he can't find the checkbook, she gently points out that if he were just a bit more organized, it would save him so much time.

And to her complete bewilderment, he doesn't melt with gratitude. In fact, the more she "helps," the worse it seems to get.

Two Different Worlds

In a woman's world, helping is one of the highest expressions of love. A good friend notices what you could do better and tells you, because she cares. But a man's world runs on something different: I've got this. It's under control. To offer advice implies he can't get the job done himself.

So when a wife offers her husband well-meant advice, she's speaking the language of love — and he's hearing the language of disrespect. What she means is "I care about you." What he hears is "You're not capable. You're only half a man."

When you direct, guide, correct, or "improve" your husband, he doesn't think, Look how much she loves me. He thinks, She believes I'm not good enough.

Socks on the Floor

A woman told Rabbi Shafier that her husband, forty years old, still leaves his socks on the bedroom floor no matter how many times she asks. He asked: Is he a nice guy? Considerate? Does he help around the house? "So here's the hard truth," he told her. "He may never change."

The answer came from a woman in Chicago. Her husband used to drape a jacket over a different dining room chair every night until his whole wardrobe was in the dining room by Thursday. She used to scream about it. It never worked. So she tried something else: every night, she quietly took the jacket upstairs and hung it up. "How's your shalom bayis now?" "Wonderful," she said.

Pushing When You Should Be Pulling

There is one way — the only way — to actually get your spouse to change. A man is locked in a room with cement walls and one door. He pushes. Nothing. He pushes harder, throws his whole body against it, day after day, until he collapses in exhaustion. Then he looks up and sees, on the bottom of the door, one word: Pull.

Stop trying to change your spouse and work instead on accepting them exactly as they are. When a husband feels respected and a wife feels cherished, both become far more eager to please the other. In a climate of respect and love, change becomes possible — which is more than pushing will ever get you.

Your Action Steps This Week

1
Catch yourself once this week — right before you correct, coach, or "improve" your spouse — and simply don't. Notice what happens instead.
2
Pick the one habit that bothers you most and quietly decide to stop fighting it. Just handle it, and watch what it does to the temperature of your home.
3
When the urge to change your spouse rises, say it to yourself: My job is to change me. My job is to accept my spouse for who they are. Then keep your eyes on your own side of the mechitzah.
