A Glimpse of Who You Could Be

This Shabbos, the Shabbos before Tisha B’Av, is called Shabbos Chazon. The name comes from the first word of the haftarah, the “vision” of Yeshayahu - the last of the three haftorahs of calamity read in the weeks leading up to Tisha B’Av. On the surface it’s a Shabbos heavy with the approach of the saddest day on the calendar. But there is a teaching, brought in the name of Rav Levi Yitzchak of Berditchev, that turns the whole thing on its head.


The word chazon, he points out, is a lashon of nevuah, of prophetic sight. And on Shabbos Chazon, he taught, every single person’s neshama is shown a vision of the third Beis Hamikdash, the rebuilt Temple, in all its future glory. We don’t see it with our physical eyes. Most of us don’t consciously register it at all. But somewhere deep in the soul, a glimpse is given. And that glimpse is precisely what allows a person to genuinely mourn on Tisha B’Av. Because you cannot truly grieve for something you’ve never seen. Only once the neshama has caught a vision of what could be does the absence of it become real enough to cry over.


The Berditchever illustrates this with a parable. A father had a precious garment sewn for his son. The boy promptly tore it to shreds. The father then made him a second garment, but the child tore that one up as well. So the father had a third garment made - this time, however, he did not permit the boy to wear it. He only allowed him to gaze upon it at appointed times, telling him: when you conduct yourself properly, you will be allowed to wear it. Over time, the father trained his son until proper conduct became second nature. And then he gave him the garment.


That is the model of Shabbos Chazon. The vision is not shown to taunt us. It is shown to train us - a glimpse of something precious enough to reach for.


Stop and sit with that idea, because it’s profound. The Berditchever is teaching that longing is born from vision. You don’t ache for a life you can’t picture. The mourning of Tisha B’Av isn’t fueled by despair, it’s fueled by a preview of something magnificent. Hashem shows the soul the destination first, and the pain of not being there yet is what moves a person to build toward it.


This is exactly the work we do as a GYE community.


So many of us know the “before.” The letter in this week’s issue describes it better than we could: the heaviness, the double life, the depression and emptiness, the sense of slipping away from who you wanted to be. When you’re living inside that shadow, it can feel permanent, like this is simply who you are and who you’ll always be. The Yetzer Hara wants you to believe you have no chazon, no vision of anything better, so that you’ll stop reaching for it.


But almost every person in this struggle has had a glimpse. Maybe it was a stretch of days or weeks when you were clean and you felt it, the clarity, the lightness, the closeness to Hashem, the quiet pride of being someone you respected. That same fighter writes: “I remember the taste of freedom I had, after fighting and winning over the yetzer hara for a tekufa... That memory will never leave me. It pulls me forward. It reminds me that a different life is possible.”


That memory is your chazon. That glimpse of freedom is the third Beis Hamikdash of your own life, and holding onto it is not a luxury. It’s the engine of the whole journey. The Berditchever is telling us that the vision comes first, and the pull toward it comes second. If you can keep the picture of who you could be in front of your eyes, the person who learns with a clear mind, who builds honest friendships, who stands under the chuppah without secrets, who feels genuinely close to Hashem, then the gap between here and there stops being a source of despair and becomes a source of drive.


But there is something the Berditchever’s teaching points to that is worth naming directly. It isn’t enough to simply want to stop a behavior. “I need to stop this because it’s bad for me” is motivation, but it doesn’t sustain. What actually creates lasting energy for change is a vision of the life you want to live. Not what you’re running from. What you’re running toward.


What kind of person do you want to be in a year? What does an ordinary day look like? How do you feel when you wake up in the morning? What do you want to accomplish? When you can picture that clearly and feel it honestly, you stop fighting against a habit and start fighting for a future. That is a different kind of fight.


GYE has a tool built exactly around this, in the motivation pillar, called Vision for Change. Inside it there is a section called Mission Statement, and a motivation bot that walks you through the questions and helps you write it up. Draw a picture of the future you actually want, make it vivid and real - and you will have something to fight for. Because when you’re fighting for something, and not just against something, the whole battle changes.


So this Shabbos Chazon, use your power of sight. When the Yetzer Hara tells you that this is as good as it gets, that you’re too far gone, that a life of real kedusha isn’t for someone like you, answer him with your chazon. Picture it. Remember the taste of the freedom you’ve already had, even for a little while. Let that glimpse of who you could be do what it was designed to do: pull you forward, out of wherever you are, toward the rebuilt Beis Hamikdash, and toward the rebuilt version of yourself. May we all be zocheh to see both, b’mheirah b’yameinu.

A Letter From a Chosson

By a GYE Fighter (and talmid of HHM)


Dear Neshama,


Let me start from the beginning, the place where my journey really began. My life was absolutely terrible; the memories alone are ripping me apart. I remember how heavy life felt then, how split I felt inside, living a double life, the worst pain of all. I remember the depression, the emptiness, the sense that I was slipping away from who I wanted to be. But I remember something else too: the taste of freedom I had, after fighting and winning over the Yetzer Hara for a tekufa, the clarity and lightness of living without this struggle. That memory will never leave me. It pulls me forward. It reminds me that a different life is possible.


I chose this path because I want to live honestly. I want to feel real. I want to stand in front of Hashem without hiding from myself. I want the confidence that I can face any nisayon and not fall. I want the kedusha that brings a person close to Hashem in the deepest way. That is the life I’m reaching for.


I also know what happens if I don’t change. I’ve seen how this struggle can darken everything, my mood, my relationships, my learning, my future. I’ve felt how it steals my joy, how it isolates me from friends, how it makes me doubt whether I can ever build a healthy marriage or a stable life. I don’t want to live in that shadow. I don’t want to lose myself.


This horrible habit once touched every corner of my life. It made conversations with friends feel impossible. It made me distracted, anxious, and ashamed. It made me wonder if I would ever make it, if I would ever feel normal, if I would ever stay on the path I believe in. It chipped away at my confidence and my hope.


But I’m not staying there.


I’m choosing a different future, one filled with real happiness, the kind that lasts. A future where I can learn with a clear mind, build honest friendships, and one day stand under a chuppah without secrets or shame. A future where I feel close to Hashem, where my life reflects the values I hold most dear.


This journey is not just about breaking a habit. It’s about becoming the person I know I can be. It’s about aligning my life with my deepest values, Yiddishkeit, family, shteiging, kedusha. It’s about building a home, a mishpacha, a life of integrity. It’s about proving to myself that I can rise above the strongest pull of my generation, something even the greatest people of earlier times never faced.


Every step I take is a sign of strength. Every day I choose this path, I grow. I become more myself. I become closer to Hashem.


Now I am a chosson, and though I’ve made massive strides in this area, it would be a lie to say I’m perfect. To say I never give a second look at a billboard. To say I would trust myself with an unfiltered device. But that doesn’t matter, because I am closer to Hashem, and I will keep getting closer.


With honesty, courage, and hope,
A GYE Fighter (and talmid of HHM)

Wisdom from the Forum: “How Important Is a Filter, and Can I Recover Without One?”

This is one of the most basic and most debated questions on the forum. Almost everyone agrees a filter matters. Where members differ is on how much it can do on its own. Here’s the range of hard-earned wisdom from the chevra.


The clearest framing comes from @bentorah.baalhabayis, who puts it simply: a filter is “only a fence to help a person not to fall, and is not actually a solution.” That’s the consensus in a sentence. A filter blocks the road, but it doesn’t do the inner work. As @bentorah.baalhabayis adds elsewhere, real recovery “only really begins” after you hit a milestone like 90 days, the filter is what protects you while the deeper change is happening, not the change itself.


But the stories of what happens without a filter are sobering, and they come from strong people, not beginners. @want to be a true jew didn’t even consider himself an addict and kept a filter on his main computer, but when he got a second PC without one, he was watching inappropriate content within two weeks. His raw question said it all: “Within two weeks I was watching inappropriate stuff, so my question is, why do I have such a weakness that I can’t control myself?” @SisonYishecha relapsed after getting an unfiltered smartphone, despite being clean for nearly ten years. The pattern is hard to miss: the moment the fence comes down, even years of sobriety can be vulnerable.


That’s why, when a strong temptation hits out of nowhere, @bentorah.baalhabayis acknowledges the flip side of his own caution: a filter can make all the difference, the few seconds of friction it creates can be exactly what carries you past the urge.


Some members do raise real reservations, and they’re worth hearing. @bentorah.baalhabayis worries that for some people, installing a filter can create a psychological pull, turning it into “forbidden fruit” that’s almost harder to resist. @i-man admits that when the tayveh was strong enough, he would find ways around the filter to get to shmutz anyway. And @embarrassedhusband captures the deepest truth of all: even after trying to stop many times, “the urge is still there to this very day.” A filter can block the screen. It can’t reach into the heart. That work is still yours to do.


The honest bottom line from the forum: it’s possible in theory, but the lived experience of member after member says the odds are steeply against you. The people who fell hardest were often the most confident. A filter won’t recover for you, and it isn’t the whole program. But it’s the fence that keeps you on the path long enough to do the real work, and almost no one regrets having it.

10 Really Dumb Mistakes Smart Couples Make

Mistake #7: Forgetting That Talk Means Something Different to Women Than It Does to Men, By Rabbi Benzion Shafier (TheShmuz.com)


Last week we learned not to try to change your spouse. This week we turn to one of the most common sources of quiet friction in a marriage, a difference so built-in that most couples never even realize it’s driving them apart: the fact that men and women talk for completely different reasons.


There’s a fundamental difference in why men and women speak. Men talk to share ideas and convey information. Women talk to connect. For a woman, conversation is the glue of the relationship, and the subject matter often matters far less than the simple fact that the two of you are talking. Women bond through talking. Men, by contrast, tend to bond through doing things together. Hashem gave a woman a naturally communicative nature because her role is to build and nurture relationships, and speech is the vehicle for building attachment.


This is the seventh really dumb mistake smart couples make: forgetting that talk means something different to women than it does to men.


“How Was Your Day?”


Rabbi Shafier illustrates it with a scene every young couple will recognize. The husband comes home. “So, dear, how was your day?” “Good.” “Just good?” “Yeah, good. It was good.” “Nothing happened today?” “No, no, it was good.”


And a quiet divide opens up. He’s thinking: What does she mean, “just good”? Did she expect a catastrophe? I said good, I mean good. Why all the questions? She’s thinking: Why isn’t he telling me about his day? Why won’t he share with me? What she actually hears in his short answers is: He wants to be distant. He doesn’t want to be connected.


Neither one is wrong, and neither one is being difficult. He’s not pushing her away, most men simply don’t “talk about their day.” And she’s not being nosy, she just wants to feel part of his life. Rabbi Shafier notes that the Chazon Ish writes that when a man leaves the house he should tell his wife where he’s going, and when he returns he should tell her what he did. These small routines feel inconsequential to him, but they’re essential to the relationship.


“We Talk All the Time” / “We Never Talk”


This is why a husband will insist “We talk all the time” while his wife says “We never talk,” and both are being completely honest. Rabbi Shafier often asks a kallah how much time she spent on the phone with friends each night before she was married. The typical answer: two or three hours. He asks the chassan the same question. The answer: maybe ten minutes. During the infatuation phase, the husband was happy to talk for hours. Then the magic wore off, and the real work began, the work of learning to communicate on purpose.


Listening, and the “Listening Sounds”


There’s a second layer to this, and it’s about listening. A husband and wife are on a date. Twenty minutes in, she says, “You’re not listening to a word I say.” Baffled, he repeats back everything she said, word for word. “See,” she says, “that proves you’re not listening.”


He’s stunned, but she’s picking up on something real. Rabbi Shafier says to listen at a kiddush sometime. On the women’s side you’ll hear: “Talk, talk, talk.” “Aaaah.” “Talk, talk, talk.” “Ooooh.” Then, “So nice talking to you!” On the men’s side: “Talk, talk, talk.” Silence. “Talk, talk, talk.” Silence. Then, “Great talking to you, bye.” Men listen in silence. Women make listening sounds, because for a woman the whole point of the conversation is to bond, and the feedback is how she shows she relates. When a wife talks to her husband, she doesn’t need him to recite her words back. She needs to know he felt what she described.


So men have to learn a kind of listening that doesn’t come naturally: listening for the emotion underneath the words. When your wife is talking, don’t just track the facts. Ask yourself what she’s feeling. What was that experience actually like for her? Try to climb into her emotional world.


The Balance


Both spouses carry responsibility here. He has to learn to connect through conversation and sharing, even though it isn’t his native language. She has to recognize that it isn’t natural for him, and that he probably can’t absorb as much talk as she can. Together they find the middle ground.


So Rabbi Shafier offers a piece of practical advice to each side. Gentlemen: if you want a strong marriage, spend time in real conversation with your wife. It doesn’t have to be a heavy heart-to-heart every time, but it should be genuine, and it helps to make it a daily ritual, over dinner, after Maariv, whenever works. And ladies: keep your friends. You probably won’t be able to share, analyze, vent, and process everything with your husband the way you can with them. Save the main things for him, but don’t expect him to single-handedly fill your entire need to talk. It’s not that he doesn’t care, and not that he doesn’t love you. He’s simply not built for it.


Your Action Steps This Week


  1. This week, when you walk in the door, offer your wife three real sentences about your day before she has to ask. Not “fine,” actual details. Watch what it does.
  2. Next time she’s telling you something, don’t reach for the facts or the fix. Instead, name the feeling underneath it to yourself, and reflect it back: “That sounds frustrating,” “You must have been so relieved.” Listen for the emotion, not the information.
  3. Set one small talking ritual as a couple, ten minutes over dinner, a few minutes before bed, and protect it. Consistency matters more than depth.
