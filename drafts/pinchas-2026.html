The Tamid: The Power of Consistent Action

Today is the 17th of Tammuz. We fast. We mourn the breach of the walls of Jerusalem. And among the tragedies the Yerushalmi records for this day, one that often gets overlooked: batel hatamid the daily sacrifice was discontinued.


During the Roman siege, the Jews had managed to keep the Tamid going despite everything. Each day they lowered golden baskets from the walls, and the Romans sent back two lambs one for the morning offering, one for the evening. The Tamid continued even under siege. Until the 17th of Tammuz, when the baskets went down and two pigs came back up. That was the end. The Tamid stopped, and shortly after, the Temple fell.


The Tamid didn’t stop because the Temple was destroyed. The Temple was destroyed because the Tamid stopped.


This is not a coincidence, and a midrash makes it explicit. The introduction to Ein Yaakov records a debate: which single verse encompasses the entire Torah? Ben Zoma says: Shema Yisrael. Ben Nanus says: love your neighbor as yourself. And Shimon ben Pazi says: “Et hakeves echad ta’aseh baboker v’et hakeves hasheni ta’aseh bein ha’arbayim” one lamb in the morning and one in the evening. The ruling goes like Ben Pazi. More central than Shema. More central than loving your neighbor.


This week’s parsha gives us the answer to why. Parshas Pinchas is where the Torah lays out the laws of the Tamid even though the Jewish people had been bringing this offering for nearly 40 years. Rashi asks why it is mentioned here. The answer: Moshe approached Hashem and asked Him to appoint a successor for the people after he was gone. Hashem’s response was striking: don’t worry about Me. I won’t forget the Jewish people. The concern is whether they will forget about Me. And Hashem’s answer to that fear is: the Tamid.


Not Shema. Not Torah study. Not even prayer. A physical action, performed twice a day, every day, without exception. Why?


Because consistent action is the only thing that actually changes a person.


You can believe the right things. Know the right things. Feel inspired, motivated, passionate. Have all the right intentions. And your life can remain completely unchanged. Ideas stay in the head. Feelings come and go. Even a single heroic act fades. The gap between what a person knows and how a person actually lives is one of the most stubborn facts of human nature. And the only bridge across that gap is consistent action.


The Sefer HaChinuch puts it in a principle: “Acharei hapeulos nimshachim halevavos” the heart is drawn after the actions. Not a single action. Actions, repeated. The Chinuch is saying that when a person does something again and again, it stops being something they do and starts being something they are. Consistent action reshapes your inside. It changes who you are at the level of character, not just behavior.


This is exactly what the Tamid is. Not a one-time declaration. Not an occasional sacrifice. The same physical action, every morning and every evening, without exception. The consistency is the point. The repetition is what keeps the connection from fading into abstraction. And the repetition is what makes it eternal, which is precisely what “Tamid” means.


For those of us working on changing a habit, this is the core principle. The problem is almost never a lack of understanding. You know what the struggle is. You know why you want to change. What is missing is not insight but consistent daily action, done whether you feel like it or not.


What does that look like? Writing on the forum. Calling a mentor. Tracking your day. Exercise. A hobby or project that builds you. The specific form matters less than the consistency. But it has to be something you actually do, every single day. Not thinking about doing it. Not planning to do it. Doing it.


And like the Tamid itself: once in the morning, once in the evening. Start the day with something. End the day with something. That consistent structure is what builds the person you are becoming. Day after day, it changes your inside. Day after day, it holds.


That is why Hashem chose the Tamid as the answer to Moshe’s deepest worry. Not a prayer, not a declaration. A consistent daily physical action. One lamb in the morning. One in the evening. Every day without exception. That is what holds.

Surrendering Is Not Giving Up

By @nathanfromny (Current streak: 37 days)


Day 37. I pushed another victory. I’m still clean.


Had a few minor flare-ups. Was thinking about the past, a few out-of-my-comfort-zone situations, weird. I kind of got reminded of my year of aveilus after my mother passed. Right after the sheloshim ended, ten more months ahead of davening for the amud three times a day. (I really, really, really didn’t like davening for the amud.) The pressure of being on time every day. The pressure of knowing that if I miss an opportunity to say kaddish, it’s not just me that loses out.


Today it’s about staying clean. Being ahead of the game and trying not to let myself down. After all, it’s not just me. It’s my wife, my kids, my nearest and dearest. I’m pushing myself out of my comfort zone spiritually, because I’ve gone a month clean and I feel the need to push in other areas too. But now I’m so tired, and that leads to letting down my guard. So all night I kept busy and away from risky situations.


I keep repeating “37” in my head (it was 36 before midnight), because in order to get to 38, I need to conquer 37. And I know I can do today, because I’ve done today 36 times already. So while my Yetzer Hara keeps yelling that FOREVER is impossible, I keep repeating “37.” Because “forever” may be impossible, I don’t know, I haven’t lived it yet. But “today” is so doable.


So here I am. Being vulnerable and maybe a little foolish for sharing my thoughts. Feeling stupid because I’m in my 50s and carrying on about feelings and emotions. But I made it, and I’m hoping someone else who’s having a hard time will get a little chizuk from this.


And one more thing: surrendering does not equal giving up. I’m putting these big, bad, overwhelming emotions into a mental box and placing it before Hashem. Hashem entrusted me with this challenge, so I’m going to put it in His hands for tonight. I’m tired, and I’m going to set it aside, confident that when I need to open that box again, Hashem will give me the energy to move forward.

Wisdom from the Forum: “How Do I Deal With the Loneliness That Triggers Me?”

Loneliness is one of the most common triggers members bring to the forum, and it hits hardest in the quiet hours. As @ScottHarvath put it honestly: “A big problem that causes me to fall is a feeling of loneliness. I’m single and not planning on starting dating for another year. So at night when I feel lonely I turn to something else, which is sometimes p and m.” Naming it that plainly is the first step. Here’s what the community had to say back.


Dig underneath the loneliness.


Loneliness is often a symptom of something deeper. @stopsurvivingstartliving shared: “It may be worthwhile to dig a little deeper and see if there’s any underlying reason for your loneliness. I only mention this because that was the reality for me, and when I discovered that, along with the amazing friends here, it changed my life. Literally.” Finding the real source can be the thing that changes everything.


And part of the answer is simply making peace with where you are right now. @nowitstime15 advises: “Accept that you are where you are, and it’s only temporary.” @eslaasos found that acceptance was what helped him with triggers like frustration and anger, the very feelings that so often ride in alongside loneliness. Acceptance isn’t resignation. It frees up the mental energy you’d otherwise spend fighting yourself.


Take it one day at a time.


When loneliness makes the road ahead look endless, shrink it down to today. @jewizard21 puts it well: “Don’t worry about if you’ll still be clean by the end of the week or if you messed up earlier. Just focus on today, and if you do that, you’ll realize by focusing on today that you’ve accumulated a lot of days individually.” @nowitstime15 echoes it simply: “It really is ODAAT” one day at a time.


Fill the empty hours with structure and movement.


Loneliness thrives in unstructured time, so build the time deliberately. @nowitstime15 offers a full playbook: “I go to the gym or exercise to take my mind off loneliness, it helps massively. Set up a set evening schedule to learn something, anything that sparks your soul. And create a bedtime routine: an hour before bed, ten-minute meditation, brush teeth, wash and moisturize your face, listen to calm music.” @onemomentatatime agrees that when a trigger hits, “physical activity helps the most.” And @realclean finds his respite in the steady rhythm of “hanging out with friends” and his “daily routine of learning and davening.”


Connect with real people, and get accountability.


The instinct of loneliness is to withdraw, but the forum’s answer is to reach out. @nowitstime15 encourages: “If there are social events, try and make an effort to go and mix with the right people.” And several members point to accountability as the single most powerful tool. @levaryeh highly recommends it for the urges that come with loneliness, and @stopsurvivingstartliving calls it the “extremely powerful tool of accountability, friends, and mentors,” pointing newcomers toward HHM (Hashem Help Me) as a mentor to reach out to.


The community itself is part of the answer. When @wannachange arrived, @eerie welcomed him with words worth holding onto: “Welcome to the most amazing family, my friend. We are here for you, we care about you, and we want to see you succeed. Keep sharing, ask, listen, read, connect, and be’ezras Hashem you’ll see a lot changing.” That connection is itself one of the strongest antidotes to loneliness there is.


Remember your “why.”


When the loneliness whispers that it isn’t worth it, come back to the reason you started. @jewizard21 keeps his reason close: “I want to have an amazing and close relationship with my basheret, and I’m fulfilling that goal by abstaining. I feel that it’s part of working on myself so that I can be the best person, to be a husband and a father.” A deeper purpose is a powerful motivator on a lonely night.


One honest word of caution.


The forum is a lifeline, but a few members airplanes22, wants taharah, shivisi, and lightning have noted that reading detailed descriptions of others’ struggles can sometimes be triggering in its own right. Be mindful of it, and use the tools available: private messaging, the chat rooms, or reporting a triggering post to the admin, as abd297 and the GYE administration suggest.


The bottom line? Loneliness feels like proof that you’re alone. It isn’t. As @nowitstime15 says: “You got this. Know that you are stronger than your thoughts.” Dig underneath it, build your evenings, reach out to real people, remember your why, and take today one day at a time. The journey is about progress, not perfection, and this community is here for you every step of the way.

10 Really Dumb Mistakes Smart Couples Make

Mistake #5: Forgetting That a Woman Needs to Be Cherished, By Rabbi Benzion Shafier (TheShmuz.com)


Last week we looked at the Rambam’s formula for a successful marriage: a man must honor his wife more than himself and love her as himself, and a woman must honor her husband greatly. But if you read it carefully, something seems off. The husband has two jobs, respect and love. The wife has only one, respect. Does that mean she’s exempt from loving her husband? Is he condemned to a loveless marriage?


The answer reveals one of the deepest truths about how men and women are built differently.


A helpmate “opposite” him


When Hashem created Chava, He called her an “ezer k’negdo,” a helpmate opposite him. The Maharal explains that k’negdo carries two meanings at once. It means “facing him” an equal partner on his level, not subservient, not second-rate. And it also means “opposite,” as in antithesis. Man and woman are not slightly different. They are opposites. They have different natures, different perspectives, different emotional priorities, different sensitivities. They will disagree not on a few things, but on most things, because by nature they are built opposite to one another.


And here’s the trap: the one thing men and women seem to share is selective amnesia. Everyone knows boys and girls are different. Then they get married and forget it entirely. A man treats his wife as if she were a man. A woman treats her husband as if he were a woman. And because each fails to understand that their spouse is fundamentally different, they can’t meet each other’s deepest needs.


The need to be cherished


One of the most basic needs a woman has is to feel deeply connected, and in marriage, that translates into a profound need to feel loved and cherished by her husband. This isn’t insecurity. It’s how she’s built. The Steipler Gaon zt”l wrote something startling about this: “The main hope of a woman is to have a husband who loves her. If she sees this isn’t so, it crushes her spirit and can be close to pikuach nefesh.”


That’s why the Torah commands a man, during the first year of marriage, to “make happy the woman whom he took.” Not to be happy together. To make her happy. Because when a man orients himself around the question “What does she need? What will please her?”, he’s constantly sending her the message that she is special, precious, number one. And once she knows that, the marriage has a foundation to succeed.


This is the answer to the Rambam. A woman isn’t commanded to love her husband, because she doesn’t need the commandment. If she feels cherished, she will love him automatically. Her love flows naturally the moment her need to be loved is met. A man, on the other hand, doesn’t have that same instinctive need for deep attachment, so he has to be commanded. For him, loving his wife is something he has to learn and work at.


His marriage and her marriage


When a couple sits in Rabbi Shafier’s office, he’ll sometimes ask the husband, “How’s your marriage?” “Baruch Hashem, good.” Then he asks the wife. “Terrible. It’s horrible.” They’re married to each other, but they’re living in two different marriages. He can feel content even when they’re not deeply connecting, because connection isn’t his core need. She can’t, because it’s hers. This is why most shalom bayis problems begin in exactly one place: the wife doesn’t feel loved.


A wise man pursues his wife


Chazal teach that it is the man who pursues the woman, and that doesn’t end at the chuppah. A husband should go on pursuing his wife for the rest of their lives, telling her again and again, in word and deed, that she is cherished, that he is there for her, that she is number one in his eyes. Many men don’t get this. “Why do you need me to keep saying it?” Because she does. Picture the little girl plucking petals off a daisy: “He loves me, he loves me not.” Rabbi Shafier says every woman goes through that stage at the start of her marriage, and “the start” means the first thirty or forty years.


This is also why the husband should be the one to initiate the date. When a man says, “If you want to go out, just book it and I’ll be there,” he thinks he’s being accommodating. But what she hears is, “He doesn’t really care.” The formula is simple: it’s the man’s job to plan the date, and the woman’s job to find the babysitter. The weekly date matters, but it isn’t the whole story. Gifts, notes, conversations, time together they all carry one message: “You are precious to me. You are important to me. You are number one.” When a woman feels that, her natural ability to love flows forth.


The Fifth Really Dumb Mistake


This is the fifth really dumb mistake smart couples make: forgetting that a woman needs to be cherished. Women need constant connection and constant reassurance of love, and it’s the husband’s job to provide it. Rabbi Shafier estimates that ninety percent of shalom bayis problems are solved when a woman feels that her husband cherishes her. It really is one of the great keys to a successful marriage.


Your Action Steps This Week


1. This week, stop waiting for your wife to ask. You plan one outing or one moment of focused attention, and tell her you set it up because you wanted time with her.


2. Send one message a day this week whose only purpose is to say “you’re on my mind.” A text from work. A note on the counter. No logistics, no agenda. Just “thinking of you.”


3. Ask yourself honestly: does my wife know, beyond any doubt, that she is number one in my eyes? If there’s any uncertainty, that’s the most important work you can do in your marriage, and it starts today.
